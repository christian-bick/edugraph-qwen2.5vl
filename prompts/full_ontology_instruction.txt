You are presented with learning material that you shall classify using three taxonomies to classify the material
across three independent dimensions:

- Area: http://edugraph.io/edu#Area
- Ability: http://edugraph.io/edu#Ability
- Scope: http://edugraph.io/edu#Scope

The rest of the prompt is divided into three parts:

A) Explanation: An explanation of the taxonomy's structure
B) Taxonomy: The taxonomy itself
C) Classification instructions: A detailed instruction on how to classify the material in the terms of the taxonomy

A) Explanation =========================================================================================================

You know that a taxonomy starts (and that another ends) when you encounter a single "#" followed by the name and URI of the
dimension like this:

- # Area (http://edugraph.io/edu#Area)
- # Ability (http://edugraph.io/edu#Ability)
- # Scope (http://edugraph.io/edu#Scope)

The taxonomies consist of two parts, an outline and a definition section.

You will know that the outline section starts when you see the header "## Outline"

You will know that the definition section starts when you see the header "## Definitions"

Between the next "---" you find an example of an outline for gastronomic terms:

---
1 Drinks (http://example.com/foo#Drinks)
1.1 Non Alcoholic (http://example.com/foo#NonAlcoholic)
1.2 Alcoholic (http://example.com/foo#Alcoholic)
1.2.1 Beer (http://example.com/foo#Beer)
1.2.2 Wine (http://example.com/foo#Wine)
2 Food (http://example.com/foo#Food)
2.1 Italian (http://example.com/foo#Italian)
2.2 French (http://example.com/foo#French)
---

Each item in the outline consists of an index, a term and a IRI consequently:

* "1 Drinks (http://example.com/foo#Drinks)" describes the term "Drinks" at index "1" with IRI "http://example.com/foo#Drinks"
* "1.1 Non Alcoholic (http://example.com/foo#NonAlcoholic)" describes the term "Non Alcoholic" at index "1.1" with IRI "http://example.com/foo#NonAlcoholic

Further, the index in the outline expresses a part-of relationship, consequently:

* "1.1" and "1.2" are part of "1" and
* "1.2.1" and "1.2.2" are part of "1.2"
* "Alcoholic" is part of "Drinks".
* "Food" has "Italian" and "French" as parts
* "Drinks" and "Food" are root elements of the hierarchy
* "Beer", "Wine", "Italian" and "French" are leaf elements of the hierarchy

Between the next "---" you find an example of definitions for gastronomic terms:

---
### 1 Drinks (http://example.com/foo#Drinks)

All types of beverages.

### 1.1 Non Alcoholic (http://example.com/foo#NonAlcoholic)

Beverages without alcohol.

### 1.2 Alcoholic (http://example.com/foo#Alcoholic)

Beverages that contain alcohol.

### 1.2.1 Beer (http://example.com/foo#Beer)

A malty alcoholic drink.

### 1.2.2 Wine (http://example.com/foo#Wine)

An alcoholic drink made of grapes.

### 2 Food (http://example.com/foo#Food)

### 2.1 Italian (http://example.com/foo#Italian)

Dishes that are typically prepared in Italy.

### 2.2 French (http://example.com/foo#French)

Dishes that are typically prepared in France.
---

Each definition has a title, definition and IRI. The title's index, term and IRI always exactly match
the outline. The outline therefore functions like an index for each term's definition. The definition of each term then
follows in the following paragraphs before a new term title is encountered, indicated by "###".

Consequently:

* "### 1 Drinks (http://example.com/foo#Drinks)" is the title with index "1" for the term "Drinks" and URI "http://example.com/foo#Drinks"
* "All types of beverages." is the definition of the term "Drinks"
* "### 1.2 Alcoholic (http://example.com/foo#Alcoholic)" is the title with index "1.2" for the term "Alcoholic" and URI "http://example.com/foo#Alcoholic
* "Beverages that contain alcohol." is the definition of the term "Alcoholic"

Sometimes, only an empty definition is provided for a term. However, when available, make use of the definition for classification.

## Definitions

### 1.1 Intuitive Mathematics (http://edugraph.io/edu#IntuitiveMathematics)

The branch of mathematics that focuses on understanding foundational math concepts through direct experience and visualization, gradually familiarizing students with increasingly formal and abstract representations of math concepts.

### 1.1.1 Proportion Sense (http://edugraph.io/edu#ProportionSense)

Proportion sense is the ability to understand and reason about proportional relationships between quantities.

### 1.1.1.1 Proportion Interaction (http://edugraph.io/edu#ProportionInteraction)

Proportion interaction is the ability to analyze and manipulate proportional relationships to solve problems.

### 1.1.1.1.1 Proportion Expression (http://edugraph.io/edu#ProportionExpression)

Proportion expression is the ability to represent proportional relationships using mathematical symbols and equations.

### 1.1.1.1.2 Proportion Identification (http://edugraph.io/edu#ProportionIdentification)

Proportion identification is the ability to recognize situations where proportional relationships exist.

### 1.1.1.1.3 Proportion Manipulation (http://edugraph.io/edu#ProportionManipulation)

Proportion manipulation is the ability to adjust or change the values in a proportion while maintaining the proportional relationship.

### 1.1.1.2 Proportion Abstraction (http://edugraph.io/edu#ProportionAbstraction)

Proportion abstraction is the ability to generalize proportional relationships and apply them to new situations.

### 1.1.1.2.1 Ratio Interpretation (http://edugraph.io/edu#RatioInterpretation)

Ratio interpretation is the ability to understand the meaning of a ratio and its relationship to the quantities involved.

### 1.1.1.2.2 Ratio Notation (http://edugraph.io/edu#RatioNotation)

Applying the system of symbols and conventions used to represent ratios.

### 1.1.1.2.3 Ratio Equivalence (http://edugraph.io/edu#RatioEquivalence)

Ratio equivalence is the understanding that different ratios can represent the same proportional relationship.

### 1.1.2 Number Sense (http://edugraph.io/edu#NumberSense)

Number sense is the intuitive understanding of numbers, their magnitudes, and how they relate to each other.

### 1.1.2.1 Base Operations (http://edugraph.io/edu#BaseOperations)

Base operations are the fundamental arithmetic actions performed on numbers that all other arithmetic operations are based one.

### 1.1.2.1.1 Sum (http://edugraph.io/edu#Sum)

Combining quantities to determine their total amount for any type of mathematical object, including numbers.

### 1.1.2.1.2 Difference (http://edugraph.io/edu#Difference)

Determining the difference in quantities of mathematical object, including numbers where difference represents the amount by which one quantity is greater or less than another.

### 1.1.2.1.3 Increment (http://edugraph.io/edu#Increment)

Raising a quantity by a certain amount.

### 1.1.2.1.4 Decrement (http://edugraph.io/edu#Decrement)

Reducing a quantity by a specific amount.

### 1.1.2.2 Numeric Comparison (http://edugraph.io/edu#NumericComparison)

Numeric comparison involves evaluating whether one number is greater than, less than, or equal to another.

### 1.1.2.2.1 Numeric Order (http://edugraph.io/edu#NumericOrder)

Numeric order refers to the arrangement of numbers in ascending or descending sequence based on their magnitude.

### 1.1.2.2.2 Numeric Identity (http://edugraph.io/edu#NumericIdentity)

Numeric identity refers to the understanding that a number remains the same regardless of its representation.

### 1.1.2.3 Numeration (http://edugraph.io/edu#Numeration)

Numeration encompasses counting, ordering, and grouping numbers.

### 1.1.2.3.1 Numeration With Rationals (http://edugraph.io/edu#NumerationWithRationals)

Numeration with rationals encompasses understanding the density of rational numbers on the number line.

### 1.1.2.3.2 Numeration With Intervals (http://edugraph.io/edu#NumerationWithIntervals)

Numeration with intervals is used to represent sets of numbers with specific limits.

### 1.1.2.3.3 Numeration With Integers (http://edugraph.io/edu#NumerationWithIntegers)

Numeration with integers includes understanding the sequence and magnitude of whole numbers.

### 1.1.2.4 Number Notation (http://edugraph.io/edu#NumberNotation)

Number notation is the system of symbols and rules used to express numerical values.

### 1.1.2.4.1 Decimal Notation (http://edugraph.io/edu#DecimalNotation)

Applying the decimal system of representing rational numbers using a base-ten place value system and a decimal point to separate whole and fractional parts.

### 1.1.2.4.2 Fraction Notation (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionNotation)

The system of symbols used to represent fractions, including the numerator and denominator.

### 1.1.2.4.3 Integer Notation (http://edugraph.io/edu#IntegerNotation)

Applying the system of symbols used to represent integers.

Example: Using the symbols -3, 0, and 12 to represent integers.

### 1.1.2.4.4 Digit Notation (http://edugraph.io/edu#DigitNotation)

Digit notation uses symbols from 0 to 9 to represent any number.

### 1.1.3 Spatial Sense (http://edugraph.io/edu#SpatialSense)

Understanding shapes, sizes, positions, and movements in two and three dimensions.

### 1.1.3.1 Spatial Modelling (http://edugraph.io/edu#SpatialModelling)

Creating mental or physical representations of objects and their spatial relationships.

### 1.1.3.1.1 Linear Shape Drawing (http://edugraph.io/edu#LinearShapeDrawing)

Linear shape drawing is the ability to create drawings or models of shapes with straight sides.

### 1.1.3.1.2 Elliptic Shape Drawing (http://edugraph.io/edu#EllipticShapeDrawing)

Elliptical shape drawing is the ability to create drawings or models of elliptical shapes.

### 1.1.3.1.3 Circular Shape Drawing (http://edugraph.io/edu#CircularShapeDrawing)

Circular shape drawing is the ability to create drawings or models of circular shapes.

### 1.1.3.2 Shape Recognition (http://edugraph.io/edu#ShapeRecognition)

Identifying and naming different geometric shapes.

### 1.1.3.2.1 Shape Symmetry (http://edugraph.io/edu#ShapeSymmetry)

Understanding how a shape can be divided into identical halves that mirror each other.

### 1.1.3.2.2 Shape Congruence (http://edugraph.io/edu#ShapeCongruence)

Understanding when two shapes are identical in size and shape, independently of their orientation.

### 1.1.3.3 Measurement (http://edugraph.io/edu#Measurement)

Measurement is the process of assigning numbers to describe the properties of objects, such as length, weight, or volume.

### 1.1.3.3.1 Measuring Time (http://edugraph.io/edu#MeasuringTime)

Measuring time involves quantifying the duration of events or the intervals between them.

### 1.1.3.3.2 Measuring Objects (http://edugraph.io/edu#MeasuringObjects)

Measuring objects involves determining their physical dimensions using appropriate tools and units.

### 1.2 Statistics (http://edugraph.io/edu#Statistics)

The branch of mathematics that provides a framework for collecting, organizing, analyzing, and interpreting data. It provides tools for quantifying and modeling randomness, variability, and uncertainty in data.

### 1.3 Calculus (http://edugraph.io/edu#Calculus)

The branch of mathematics that provides tools for analyzing rates of change and accumulation. It deals with instantaneous rates of change that affect the slopes of curves, as well as with accumulation that affects areas under curves.

### 1.4 Computational Mathematics (http://edugraph.io/edu#ComputationalMathematics)

The branch of mathematics that involves the development and application of mathematical methods and algorithms for solving problems using computers.

### 1.5 Analysis (http://edugraph.io/edu#Analysis)

The branch of mathematics that deals with continuous change and investigates concepts like limits, continuity, derivatives, integrals, and infinite series to study functions, their properties, and their behavior.

### 1.6 Number Theory (http://edugraph.io/edu#NumberTheory)

The branch of mathematics that studies the properties of numbers, particularly integers (whole numbers). It explores things like prime numbers, divisibility, and number patterns.

### 1.7 Logic (http://edugraph.io/edu#Logic)

The branch of mathematics that studies reasoning and valid arguments. It provides a framework for determining whether something is true or false based on given premises. It is the foundation for constructing proofs, ensuring that conclusions follow logically from their premises.

### 1.8 Algebra (http://edugraph.io/edu#Algebra)

The branch of mathematics that generalizes arithmetics and where letters represent unknown numbers, allowing us to explore patterns, solve equations, and model real-world situations.

### 1.9 Set Theory (http://edugraph.io/edu#SetTheory)

The branch of mathematics that studies sets, which are collections of objects. It provides a fundamental language and tools for defining and working with mathematical objects.

### 1.10 Arithmetic (http://edugraph.io/edu#Arithmetic)

The branch of mathematics that deals with the properties and manipulation of numbers, including addition, subtraction, multiplication and division.

### 1.10.1 Rationals Arithmetic (http://edugraph.io/edu#RationalsArithmetic)

Performing arithmetic operations on rational numbers (numbers that can be expressed as a fraction).

### 1.10.1.1 Fraction Arithmetic (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionArithmetic)

Performing arithmetic operations on fractions.

### 1.10.1.1.1 Fraction Simplification (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionSimplification)

Reducing a fraction to its simplest form by dividing the numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor.

Example: 12/18 simplifies to 2/3 by dividing both by 6

### 1.10.1.1.2 Fraction Addition (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionAddition)

Adding two or more fractions, often requiring finding a common denominator.

Example: 1/4 + 3/8 = 2/8 + 3/8 = 5/8

### 1.10.1.1.3 Fraction Root (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionRoot)

Finding the fraction that, when raised to a certain power, produces a given fraction.

Example: The square root of 9/16 is 3/4 because (3/4) ^ 2 = 9/16

### 1.10.1.1.4 Fraction Subtraction (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionSubtraction)

Finding the difference between two fractions, often requiring finding a common denominator.

Example: 3/4 - 1/8 = 6/8 - 1/8 = 5/8

### 1.10.1.1.5 Fraction Division (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionDivision)

Dividing one fraction by another, which is equivalent to multiplying by the reciprocal of the second fraction.

Example: (2/3) / (1/4) = (2/3) * (4/1) = 8/3

### 1.10.1.1.6 Fraction Multiplication (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionMultiplication)

Multiplying two or more fractions by multiplying the numerators and the denominators.

Example: (2/5) * (3/7) = 6/35

### 1.10.1.1.7 Fraction Logarithm (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionLogarithm)

Finding the exponent to which a base (a fraction) must be raised to produce a given fraction.

### 1.10.1.1.8 Lowest Common Denominator (http://edugraph.io/edu#LowestCommonDenominator)

Finding the smallest common multiple of the denominators of two or more fractions.

Example: The lowest common denominator of 1/4 and 1/6 is 12.

### 1.10.1.1.9 Fraction Exponentiation (http://edugraph.io/edu#FractionExponentiation)

Raising a fraction to a power.

Example: (1/2) ^ 3 = (1/2) * (1/2) * (1/2) = 1/8

### 1.10.1.2 Decimal Arithmetic (http://edugraph.io/edu#DecimalArithmetic)

Performing arithmetic operations on decimals (numbers represented with a decimal point).

### 1.10.1.2.1 Decimal Multiplication (http://edugraph.io/edu#DecimalMultiplication)

### 1.10.1.2.2 Decimal Division (http://edugraph.io/edu#DecimalDivision)

### 1.10.1.2.5 Decimal Logarithm (http://edugraph.io/edu#DecimalLogarithm)

### 1.10.1.2.6 Decimal Notation (http://edugraph.io/edu#DecimalNotation)

### 1.10.1.2.7 Decimal Subtraction (http://edugraph.io/edu#DecimalSubtraction)

### 5 Analytical Capability (http://edugraph.io/edu#AnalyticalCapability)

The ability to examine information critically, break it down into its component parts, and identify patterns and relationships. ### 5.1.1 Logical Reasoning (http://edugraph.io/edu#LogicalReasoning)

The process of using reason and evidence to arrive at valid conclusions. It involves analyzing information, identifying patterns, and making inferences based on established rules or principles. Logical reasoning can be divided into two main types: deductive reasoning, where conclusions are guaranteed to be true if the premises are true, and non-deductive reasoning, where conclusions are likely but not guaranteed.

### 5.1.1.1 Deductive Reasoning (http://edugraph.io/edu#DeductiveReasoning)

A type of reasoning where the conclusion is guaranteed to be true if the premises are true. It moves from general principles to specific conclusions, using logic and rules of inference to establish certainty. Deductive reasoning is often used in mathematics, logic, and other fields where absolute certainty is desired.

### 5.1.1.1.1 Axiom Definition (http://edugraph.io/edu#AxiomDefinition)

The ability to identify and clearly state the fundamental assumptions or self-evident truths that serve as the foundation for a deductive system. This involves recognizing basic principles, defining key terms, and establishing the starting points for logical reasoning. Axiom definition is crucial for building a coherent and consistent framework for deductive reasoning, ensuring that all conclusions follow logically from the initial assumptions.

### 5.1.1.1.2 Logical Inference (http://edugraph.io/edu#LogicalInference)

The ability to draw valid conclusions from given premises or axioms using deductive reasoning. This involves applying rules of inference, such as modus ponens or modus tollens, to derive new statements that necessarily follow from the initial assumptions.

### 5.1.1.2 Non Deductive Reasoning (http://edugraph.io/edu#NonDeductiveReasoning)

A type of logical reasoning where the conclusion is likely or probable, but not guaranteed, based on the given premises. Unlike deductive reasoning, which draws certain conclusions, non-deductive reasoning deals with uncertainty and degrees of support. It encompasses various forms of reasoning, including abductive, analogical, and inductive reasoning, each with its own approach to drawing inferences and making generalizations.

### 5.1.1.2.1 Inductive Reasoning (http://edugraph.io/edu#InductiveReasoning)

A form of logical inference that involves drawing general conclusions from specific observations or examples. It moves from specific instances to broader generalizations. Inductive reasoning is often used in scientific inquiry to develop hypotheses and theories based on empirical evidence.

### 5.1.1.2.2 Analogical Reasoning (http://edugraph.io/edu#AnalogicalReasoning)

A form of logical inference that involves identifying similarities between two or more situations or objects and then using those similarities to draw conclusions or make predictions about one of them. It relies on the idea that if two things are alike in some respects, they are likely to be alike in other respects as well. Analogical reasoning is often used in problem-solving, decision-making, and creative thinking.

### 5.1.1.2.3 Abductive Reasoning (http://edugraph.io/edu#AbductiveReasoning)

A form of logical inference that starts with an observation or set of observations and then seeks to find the simplest and most likely explanation for the observations. It involves making an educated guess or hypothesis based on incomplete information. Abductive reasoning is often used in everyday life and in fields like science and detective work to generate possible explanations for phenomena.

### 5.1.2 Logical Processing (http://edugraph.io/edu#LogicalProcessing)

Understand and applying a series of steps or operations performed in a specific order to achieve a desired outcome. These steps are based on principles of logic and reasoning, ensuring that each action is justified and contributes to the overall goal.

### 5.1.2.1 Procedure Application (http://edugraph.io/edu#ProcedureApplication)

The ability to select and utilize the correct procedure for a given task or problem. This involves analyzing the situation, identifying the relevant features, and choosing the most appropriate procedure from a repertoire of known procedures. Procedure application requires understanding the purpose and constraints of different procedures and their suitability for various contexts.

### 5.1.2.1.1 Procedure Identification (http://edugraph.io/edu#ProcedureIdentification)
